Purple Hibiscus, Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

★ ★ ★ ★ ★Rated 5 out of 5 stars
August 18, 2025 · 4 min read

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's ‘Purple Hibiscus’ is a powerful story of a young Nigerian girl raised in a wealthy, yet abusive Catholic household. Set roughly twenty years after Nigeria gained independence from British colonialism in 1960, Adichie explores the effects of colonialism, civil war and a shift in religious beliefs within a single family. She depicts the family’s struggle for a modern identity against the backdrop of a devastating military coup.

Shuggie Bain, Douglas Stuart

★ ★ ★ ★ ☆Rated 4 out of 5 stars
July 16, 2025 · 3 min read

Douglas Stuart’s ‘Shuggie Bain’ follows the childhood of a young boy, Shuggie, and the struggles he faced growing up in a working class background of 1980s Scotland. Despite his desperate attempts to fit in and behave like a ‘real boy’, his homosexuality is apparent leading to bullying and his own insecurity. His mother, Agnes, is an alcoholic, abandoned by almost everyone in her life, from her husband to her eldest children. Yet, Shuggie withstands her temperamental personality and unreliable mothering attributes, despite his own happiness and security. It is a story of loss, hardship and the acceptance of one’s identity. It is a story of a young boy that is forced to grow up too quickly.

The Bell Jar, Sylvia Plath

★ ★ ★ ★ ★Rated 5 out of 5 stars
June 15, 2025 · 2 min read

Written just months before her suicide, Plath’s ‘The Bell Jar’ tells the story of ambitious student Esther Greenwood as her dreams of becoming a poet are put on hold due to a mental health crisis. Plath’s writing is clear, impactful and full of heart wrenching experiences. Plath entwines ambition, dissociation between mind and body and womanhood to create a compelling tale of real experiences.
