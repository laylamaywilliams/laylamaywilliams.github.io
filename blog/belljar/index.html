The Bell Jar, Sylvia Plath

★ ★ ★ ★ ★Rated 5 out of 5 stars
June 15, 2025 · 2 min read

Written just months before her suicide, Plath’s ‘The Bell Jar’ tells the story of ambitious student Esther Greenwood as her dreams of becoming a poet are put on hold due to a mental health crisis. Plath’s writing is clear, impactful and full of heart wrenching experiences. Plath entwines ambition, dissociation between mind and body and womanhood to create a compelling tale of real experiences.

One of my favourite moments of the novel is Plath’s description of the fig tree. Arguably, she uses this as a metaphor for the abundance of choices people can experience in life and how this can often lead to overwhelm. I think what Plath is trying to suggest is that the combination of ambition and a lack of direction can often lead to feelings of helplessness and anxiety. As a character who has worked relentlessly all of her life, Esther consequently suffers from having to choose between a number of ‘wonderful future[s]’ but is incapable of choosing just one ‘fig’. After some research, I discovered that ‘the green fig tree’ is possibly an allusion to a biblical story. In this story, Jesus and his disciples come across a fig tree that had many green leaves. However, upon drawing closer, the tree only had leaves and no fruit. Perhaps this suggests that, deep down, the character of Esther knows that none of these future possibilities will be fulfilled. Consequently, Esther’s fear of making the right decision overrides a fear of not making a decision at all, leaving her stranded and ‘starving to death’.

What I loved most about ‘The Bell Jar’ was Plath’s prose style. She uses rich figurative language and her writing is full of expertly crafted metaphors. This is ultimately what makes the novel engaging, sorrowful and cathartic. Although it is a difficult read, I was immersed in the world Plath creates and hence, I would give it five stars. Everyone should read this.
