War Horse, National Theatre

★ ★ ★ ★ ★Rated 5 out of 5 stars
June 17, 2025 · 3 min read

On the 11th of June 2025, I saw the National Theatre’s ‘War Horse’ for the second time. Adapted for the stage by Nick Strafford, ‘War Horse’ is a compelling story of a boy’s love for his horse set against the devastating backdrop of World War One.

What makes ‘War Horse’ a play unlike any other, is their spectacular use of puppetry. Designed by the Handspring Puppet Company, Joey and Topthorn, the leading horses, come alive on stage. This is, in large, due to the incredible actors in control of the puppets. Each horse is controlled by a group of three actors who work together to create the horse’s character. The detail and concentration that goes into controlling these horses is unimaginable. From the changes in breath to the subtle stamp of a hoof or flick of a tail, the horses become alive in every way. I frequently had to remind myself that they were not real horses! Another way in which the puppets become alive is through the actors' interactions with them, both puppeteers and characters. By simply looking at the puppet, the actor commandeering the head of the horse drew the audience’s attention to the horses encouraging them to focus on the animal rather than the actor. Furthermore, the character’s interactions with the horses evoke a range of emotions from the audience depending on their relationship with the horse. Consequently, ‘War Horse’ is an original, immersive experience that I would see time and time again.

The fixed set of ‘War Horse’ consists of a projection screen designed to look like a torn piece of parchment. Throughout the play, this screen is used to project both heart-warming and heart-breaking images. For instance, when the audience sees Albert’s and Joey’s relationship begin to flourish, time is made prominent through the sketches of the passing seasons. This alongside the singer’s incredible voice and the emotive music creates a beautiful, aesthetically appealing scene. Another use of projection that stood out to me was a moment in which blood turned into poppies after the death of many of Albert’s squad in Act Two. This imaginative reminder of those lost to the war brought tears to the eyes of all those watching.

Undeniably, the show would not be what it is without the incredible backstage crew. Having been lucky enough to gain insight to what goes on backstage, I was made aware of the fast-paced, precise happenings that ensure each performance is at its best. The process of props being taken from and handed to the actors at the moments required was methodical and rapid. Watching the play from beneath the stage with the follow-spot crew or from behind the curtains with the stage manager heightened my appreciation for the show as a whole.

It would not be fair to write this review without mentioning the remarkable cast. This talented group of people are what make ‘War Horse’ a magical and unique performance. They are the force that immerse the audience into the world of Albert and Joey, take us back to the early 20th century and create a truly breathtaking experience.

The National Theatre’s ‘War Horse’ is certainly a five star show. I cannot wait to see it again.
